Ferdowsi

: Abul-Qasem Ferdowsi (c. 940–1020 CE) was an Iranian poet whose life's work was devoted to chronicling the epic tales of ancient Persia. By composing the Shahnameh (Book of Kings) almost entirely in Persian—with minimal Arabic loanwords—he safeguarded the Persian language at a time when Arabic was becoming dominant in the region. His choice to write in Persian not only created a literary masterpiece but also revitalized the cultural identity of Iran, ensuring that the language would continue to flourish for centuries to come.

Shahnameh

Just as the Iliad and Odyssey are cornerstones of Greek mythology, the Shahnameh occupies a similar place in the Persian cultural psyche. The Shahnameh has 62 stories, written in 50,000 rhyming couplets, and was told in 990 chapters. The Shahnameh depicts much of how storytelling was during the era. It consists of three parts: the mythical, heroic, and historical ages. These signify the cycles called Pishdayan, Keyaniyan, and Sassanian, respectively.

Socio-Politicals and all that jazz

Ferdowsi wrote during a time when Persia was under the influence of the Samanid and later Ghaznavid dynasties. Arabs had overthrown the Sassanid Empire, and Arabic was increasingly prevalent in official settings. This transitional period made preserving Persian culture crucial. Through glorifying Persian heroes and the country's pre-Islamic past, Ferdowsi effectively staged a literary resistance to cultural assimilation, inspiring renewed pride in Persian heritage.
